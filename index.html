About

I am a PhD candidate in Political Science at the University of California, Berkeley. I study disability politics, social movements, and human rights activism in African countries. My research examines how disability rights movements emerge, how advocacy organizations make claims on the state, and how official statistics shape political recognition.

My work examines how disability rights movements emerge, how disabled persons organizations engage with the state, and how institutions such as national censuses shape political visibility and citizenship.

More broadly, I am interested in comparative politics, African politics, political identity, state formation, and questions of inclusion and accessibility.

Research

My dissertation examines the emergence and effectiveness of disability rights movements in Africa, with a focus on how disabled persons organizations have shaped state recognition through national censuses, rights legislation, and social protection systems.

The project combines qualitative and quantitative methods, including archival research, interviews, social network analysis, and event-history analysis to examine variation in disability recognition across African countries.

My broader research interests include disability politics, African politics, political representation, state legibility, social movements, and comparative political development.

Teaching & Methods Training

I have taught and supported courses in quantitative methods, comparative politics, African politics, and research design at the University of California, Berkeley.

I have also worked with Berkeley’s Data Science and Social Sciences research community as both an instructor and consultant, supporting students and researchers in areas such as data analysis, computational social science, and research methodology.

I am committed to making computational social science and academic research more accessible to diverse student communities.

Disability Rights Advocacy

My work bridges academic research and public engagement on disability rights, accessibility, political inclusion, and representation.

I am committed to supporting broader conversations about disability, citizenship, accessibility, and social justice while promoting more inclusive approaches to research, data collection, and public policy.
